## The Short Version

Anansi Technology Corporation is proposing a pilot program with GCNA that would give Grenadian nutmeg farmers something they've never had: **instant liquidity on the surplus value of their deliveries.**

Nothing about GCNA's current operations changes. Farmers still deliver nutmeg. GCNA still weighs, inspects, processes, and pays the EC$ advance. GCNA still sells to overseas buyers.

What changes is what happens *after* delivery: farmers receive a digital record of their surplus claim that they can hold (and receive the surplus when it arrives, as usual) or sell immediately if they need cash now.

GCNA gets a transparency and record-keeping upgrade at no cost. Farmers get faster access to money. Global buyers get a new way to invest in Grenadian nutmeg. Grenada gets attention as a leader in agricultural innovation.

Anansi builds and maintains the technology. GCNA provides the nutmeg and the institutional trust.

## 2. The Opportunity for GCNA

### What GCNA Does Well (and What Doesn't Change)

GCNA has operated for decades as the backbone of Grenada's nutmeg industry. The cooperative model works: farmers deliver, receive advances, and trust that GCNA will process, sell, and distribute surplus fairly.

**None of this changes under this proposal.**

- Farmers still deliver to GCNA receiving stations
- GCNA still weighs, inspects, and grades
- GCNA still pays the EC$ advance on delivery
- GCNA still processes, pools, and sells to overseas buyers
- GCNA still controls physical custody at every stage

### What Could Be Better

Based on our research and conversations with farmers and agricultural stakeholders in Grenada, we've identified several areas where current operations — while functional — leave value on the table:

**For farmers:**
- Surplus payments take months to arrive after delivery
- The amount of surplus is often unclear until it arrives
- Farmers who need cash between delivery and surplus payment have no options
- There is no way to "cash out" a surplus claim early

**For GCNA:**
- Record-keeping at receiving stations is manual and paper-based
- Tracking individual farmer contributions across pools is labor-intensive
- There is limited transparency into pool status for farmers or external stakeholders
- GCNA has no mechanism to attract global investment into Grenadian nutmeg

**For Grenada:**
- The nutmeg industry is invisible to global capital markets
- Diaspora Grenadians who want to invest back home have no agricultural investment pathway
- Grenada's agricultural innovation story is underrepresented globally

### What Spice Adds

Spice addresses all of these without requiring GCNA to change its core operations:

**For farmers:** After delivering nutmeg and receiving their EC$ advance, farmers also receive a digital record (called NUTMG) representing their share of the pool's future surplus. They can hold it and receive the surplus when GCNA sells — exactly as they do today. Or, if they need cash now, they can sell their NUTMG to a buyer for immediate USDC (a stable digital currency pegged to the US dollar), which converts to EC$ in their account.

**For GCNA:** A digital dashboard replaces paper records at receiving stations. Every delivery, every weight, every grade is recorded digitally with photos and documents. Pool status, farmer contributions, and surplus distributions are tracked automatically. GCNA gains a complete, auditable digital record of all operations — useful for reporting to government, partners, and auditors.

**For Grenada:** Grenada becomes visible to global agricultural investors. Diaspora Grenadians can invest small amounts directly in the nutmeg they grew up with. International media coverage of the innovation positions Grenada as a forward-thinking agricultural economy.

## 3. How It Works — Step by Step

### Step 1: Normal Delivery (Nothing Changes)

James arrives at the GCNA receiving station in Gouyave with 47 bags of nutmeg. The receiving officer weighs and inspects the delivery. James receives his EC$ advance.

**This step is identical to today.**

### Step 2: Digital Recording (New — At the Receiving Station)

The GCNA receiving officer opens the Spice dashboard on a computer or tablet at the station. They log in with their GCNA email address (no special software to install — it runs in a web browser).

They enter:
- James's farmer ID
- Number of bags (47)
- Total weight (564 kg)
- Grade (A)
- They snap a photo of the warehouse receipt

The system creates a digital record and issues 564 NUTMG to James's account (one per kilogram). James's phone receives a notification: "You delivered 564 kg of nutmeg. Estimated surplus value: $312."

**Time added to the receiving process: approximately 2 minutes.**

### Step 3: James Has Options (New — On James's Phone)

James now has choices he didn't have before:

**Option A — Wait for surplus (same as today, but transparent):** James holds his NUTMG. When GCNA sells the pool, surplus is automatically distributed to his account. He can see the pool status and estimated value at any time on his phone.

**Option B — Cash out now (new):** James needs money for school fees. He opens the app and taps "Withdraw." The system sells some of his NUTMG to a global buyer and deposits USDC into James's account, which he converts to EC$. He receives cash within hours, not months.

**Option C — Hold some, sell some:** James sells half his NUTMG for immediate cash and keeps the rest for the surplus distribution later.

### Step 4: GCNA Sells the Pool (Nothing Changes Operationally)

GCNA sells the pooled nutmeg to an overseas buyer, exactly as it does today. After deducting costs and the advances already paid, GCNA calculates the surplus.

### Step 5: Surplus Distribution (New — Automatic and Transparent)

Instead of manually calculating and distributing surplus to individual farmers, GCNA deposits the surplus amount into the Spice system. The system automatically calculates each farmer's share based on their NUTMG holdings and distributes it to their accounts.

Farmers who already sold their NUTMG don't receive surplus (the buyer who purchased their NUTMG does — they took on that position). Farmers who held their NUTMG receive their full surplus share, automatically.

**What this replaces:** Manual surplus calculation and distribution. Paper records. Delays. Opacity.

## 4. What GCNA Needs to Do

### During the Pilot

GCNA's involvement is minimal and focused on what GCNA already does:

1. **Designate one receiving station** for the pilot (Gouyave recommended — highest volume)
2. **Designate one GCNA staff member** as the Spice admin (trained by Anansi — training takes approximately 2 hours)
3. **Record deliveries digitally** using the Spice dashboard (adds ~2 minutes per delivery to existing process)
4. **Upload warehouse receipts** (photo or scan of existing documents)
5. **Deposit surplus** into the Spice system when a pool is sold (one additional step at sale time)
6. **Provide monthly inventory attestation** (a signed statement confirming physical nutmeg in the warehouse matches digital records)

### What GCNA Does NOT Need to Do

- Buy, install, or maintain any technology (Anansi provides everything)
- Change the EC$ advance payment process
- Change how nutmeg is processed or sold
- Handle any digital currency
- Interact with any blockchain directly
- Explain technology to farmers (Anansi handles farmer onboarding)
- Take any financial risk

## 6. Pilot Scope

### What We're Proposing

A 6-month pilot at one GCNA receiving station, with a small group of participating farmers.

| Parameter | Scope |
|---|---|
| Duration | 6 months |
| Receiving station | 1 (Gouyave recommended) |
| Participating farmers | 10-50 (voluntary — no farmer is required to participate) |
| Nutmeg lots | 1-3 lots during the pilot (a "lot" is one batch cycle: deliveries in → sold overseas → surplus distributed) |
| Technology cost to GCNA | Zero |
| Change to EC$ advance process | None |
| Change to GCNA sales process | Minimal (one additional step at surplus distribution) |

### Success Criteria

At the end of the pilot, we will evaluate together:

1. Did the digital recording system work reliably?
2. Did farmers receive their surplus faster and more transparently?
3. Did any farmer successfully access early liquidity by selling NUTMG?
4. Did the system create any problems for GCNA operations?
5. Does GCNA want to expand to additional stations?

If the answer to questions 1-3 is yes and question 4 is no, we discuss expansion. If not, the pilot concludes with no obligation.

## 7. Revenue Model — How Anansi Earns

Transparency about our business model:

Anansi earns revenue from small platform fees charged when the system processes transactions (such as when a farmer sells their NUTMG or when surplus is distributed). These fees are paid by the system, not by GCNA or the farmer — they are embedded in the transaction and are typically less than 1% of the transaction value.

Anansi does not earn money from:
- GCNA's nutmeg sales
- Farmer advance payments
- GCNA operations in any form
- Trading nutmeg or any commodity

Anansi is a software company. We earn from software usage, not from commodity trading.

## 8. Risk and Compliance

### What We've Considered

**Regulatory:** Anansi is working with legal counsel experienced in digital asset regulation. The surplus claim tokens (NUTMG) are structured to comply with applicable securities regulations. Caribbean regulatory frameworks for digital assets are evolving, and we are designing within them — not around them.

**Operational:** The Spice system is designed so that if it goes offline or Anansi ceases operations, GCNA's core business is completely unaffected. Farmers still have their EC$ advances. GCNA still has their nutmeg. The digital records are supplementary to — not a replacement for — GCNA's existing processes.

**Technology:** The platform is built on established, audited technology. All code is reviewed by independent security auditors before deployment. Farmer data is encrypted and protected.

**Farmer protection:** No farmer is required to participate. No farmer loses their EC$ advance. No farmer is worse off than they are today. Participation only adds options — it never removes existing benefits.

## 9. Why Grenada, Why GCNA, Why Now

**Why Grenada:** Grenada is "The Spice Isle" — there is no more natural starting point for a platform called Spice. Nutmeg is Grenada's signature crop and its cooperative structure (GCNA) provides exactly the kind of trusted institutional anchor that this technology requires.

**Why GCNA:** GCNA has the trust of farmers, the physical infrastructure, and the institutional authority to make this work. Without GCNA, this is just software. With GCNA, it's a real solution for real farmers.

**Why now:** Agricultural technology and digital finance are converging globally. Countries across Asia, Africa, and Latin America are experimenting with similar systems. Grenada has an opportunity to lead in the Caribbean — and GCNA has an opportunity to show that cooperatives can be at the forefront of innovation, not behind it.
